Protesters have gathered at Wembley Stadium during the Indian Prime Minister's visit to Britain.

Tens of thousands of Narendra Modi fans packed the stadium for a huge rally on Friday, as part of the first visit to the UK by an Indian Prime Minister for a decade. However, a crowd of protesters also gathered outside, voicing their opposition to human rights abuses in India.On Thursday crowds also gathered to protest at Downing Street as the Indian Prime Minister met for talks with David Cameron.

Hundreds of protesters from across the UK have taken part in the demonstrations, and have been sharing photographs and messages on Twitter, using the hashtags #HerVoice and #ModiNotWelcome. Among the campaigners taking part in the protest at Wembley was Leslee Udwin, director of BBC documentary India’s Daughter, which was banned by the Indian government in March. She tweeted: "At Wembley Stadium with serious messages to PM Modi. Join with us in the protesting the ban on India's daughter."

Rahila Gupta, from Southall Black Sisters, said women from the organisation had joined the protests to object to a rise in religious fundamentalism in India. She told the Standard: "We've been concerned about the rise of religious fundamentalism within our community. "It has a direct impact on freedom of women and their rights. "Our work is to help women who are escaping violence, and we've seen how conservative and religious values reaffirm restrictions on women, and make it hard for them to leave violent marriages, for example.

"The rise of Hindu fundamentalism has a direct impact on our communities here, it narrows freedoms that they have. "We stand for secular values and work with women of all religions and none, they see that there is more that unites them than divides them. "Religion is often used as an excuse to perpetrate violence, and we felt it was really important to protest against Modi's visit." The huge rally came after Mr Modi lunched with the Queen at Buckingham Palace and attends further meetings with business leaders as part of a trade drive.

After lunch, he was shown a selection of items from the Palace's collection, and exchanged gifts with the monarch. Mr Modi smiled as he was presented with a silver dish and signed photos of the Queen and the Duke of Edinburgh.

He gave the Queen photographs of her visit to India in 1961 and a gift box including Darjeeling tea from West Bengal, organic honey from Jammu and Kashmir, and silk Tanchoi scarves from his parliamentary constituency of Varanasi.
